01 вҖ” Passive Components: Resistors, Capacitors, Inductors

The foundation of every circuit you’ll ever build or debug

Prerequisite: Basic algebra (V=IR, that’s it) Unlocks: Pull-up/pull-down resistors, decoupling, voltage dividers, RC filters, power budgeting

Why Should I Care? (STM32/Jetson Project Context)

Every single wire in your SPI bridge has passive components on it: - Resistors: Pull-ups on CS lines keep slaves deselected during boot. Voltage dividers scale 5V signals to 3.3V. Current-limiting resistors protect GPIOs. - Capacitors: 100nF decoupling caps next to the STM32 prevent brownout during DMA bursts. Bulk 10ВөF caps smooth the 3.3V rail. RC filters on analog sensor inputs prevent aliasing. - Inductors: Ferrite beads on power lines keep switching noise from reaching the MCU. The Jetson’s DC-DC converter uses inductors internally.

If you skip this chapter, you cannot debug power-related SPI glitches. Half of the “my SPI drops frames randomly” bugs are really “my power supply sags when the MCU is busy” bugs.

PART 1 вҖ” RESISTORS

1.1 What a Resistor Physically IS

A resistor is a component that opposes the flow of electric current. It converts electrical energy into heat.

The highway analogy: Imagine current (electrons) as cars on a highway. A resistor is a section of road that narrows from 4 lanes to 1 lane. The same number of cars must get through, but they slow down and pile up. The wider the bottleneck (lower resistance), the more cars flow. The narrower (higher resistance), the fewer.

Where the analogy breaks down: in a real highway, cars queue up. In a resistor, electrons don’t “wait” вҖ” instead, a voltage difference builds up across the resistor (like water pressure building behind a constriction). This voltage drop is what Ohm’s law describes.

Physically, resistors are made from: - Carbon film: A thin carbon layer on a ceramic rod. Cheap, Вұ5% tolerance. Brown/black cylinders with color bands. - Metal film: A thin metal (nickel-chromium) spiral on ceramic. Better: Вұ1% tolerance, lower noise. Blue body. - SMD (Surface Mount): Tiny rectangular chips soldered flat on PCBs. The “0402” and “0603” you see everywhere. Marked with 3-digit codes (e.g., “103” = 10 Г— 10Ві = 10kО©). - Wire-wound: Actual wire on a bobbin. For high power (5W+). Used in power supplies.

1.2 Ohm’s Law вҖ” The One Equation That Rules Them All

    V = I Г— R

    V = voltage across the resistor (Volts)
    I = current through the resistor (Amps)
    R = resistance (Ohms, О©)

Rearranged: I = V/R and R = V/I

Why should I care? Every time you add a component to a circuit, you need Ohm’s law to calculate the current flowing, the voltage dropped, or the resistor value needed. It’s the print() of electronics.

Solution: 1. Voltage across the resistor: Vr = 3.3V - 2.0V = 1.3V (the LED “uses” 2V, the rest falls across R) 2. Desired current: I = 10mA = 0.010A 3. Ohm’s law: R = V/I = 1.3V / 0.010A = 130О©

1.3 Voltage Dividers

A voltage divider takes a high voltage and produces a lower voltage using two resistors.

Formula:

    Vout = Vin Г— R2 / (R1 + R2)

ELI15 intuition: Think of R1 and R2 as two sections of a garden hose, the same water (current) flows through both. The pressure (voltage) drops proportionally across each section. If both hoses are the same length (R1 = R2), the pressure at the midpoint is exactly half.

Worked Example: Scaling 5V to 3.3V for an ADC input

A sensor outputs 0-5V, but your STM32 ADC accepts 0-3.3V maximum.

Target ratio: Vout/Vin = 3.3/5 = 0.66

Choose R2 = 20kО©, solve for R1:

    0.66 = 20k / (R1 + 20k)
    R1 + 20k = 20k / 0.66 = 30.3k
    R1 = 10.3k вҶ’ use 10kО© standard value

Check: Vout = 5 Г— 20k / (10k + 20k) = 5 Г— 0.667 = 3.33V вң“

The loading problem: A voltage divider assumes no current is drawn from Vout. If you connect a load that draws current, the output voltage drops. For the ADC example above, the STM32 ADC input impedance is ~50kО© during sampling. This appears in parallel with R2:

    R2_effective = R2 вҲҘ R_load = (20k Г— 50k) / (20k + 50k) = 14.3kО©
    Vout_loaded = 5 Г— 14.3k / (10k + 14.3k) = 2.94V  вҶҗ lower than expected!

Fix: Use lower resistor values (1kО© + 2kО©) so the load doesn’t matter, OR add a buffer (op-amp voltage follower) between the divider and the ADC. See chapter 03.

1.4 Pull-Up and Pull-Down Resistors

Why should I care? SPI CS lines need pull-ups. I2C SDA/SCL need pull-ups. Reading a button on a GPIO needs a pull-up or pull-down. Missing pull-ups cause ~30% of “my bus doesn’t work” bugs.

What “Floating” Means

A CMOS input (like a GPIO pin configured as input) has extremely high impedance вҖ” think 10MО© to 1TО©. It draws essentially zero current. This means that if you leave it disconnected, its voltage is determined by whatever stray charge/coupling is around.

Internal Pull-Ups in MCUs

Most MCUs have internal pull-up/pull-down resistors you can enable in software. On STM32, they’re typically 30kО©вҖ“50kО©. This is fine for buttons but TOO WEAK for I2C (can’t charge bus capacitance fast enough at 400kHz). Always use external pull-ups for I2C.

gpio_pin_configure(dev, PIN, GPIO_INPUT | GPIO_PULL_UP);

1.5 Power Dissipation вҖ” When Resistors Catch Fire

Formula:

    P = IВІ Г— R     (power in watts)
    P = VВІ / R     (equivalent form)
    P = V Г— I      (most general form)

Worked Example: Why a 100О© Resistor on 12V Gets Hot

    I = V/R = 12V / 100О© = 0.12A = 120mA
    P = IВІ Г— R = 0.12ВІ Г— 100 = 1.44W

A standard through-hole resistor is rated for 0.25W (1/4 watt). You’re dissipating 1.44W вҖ” that’s 5.8Г— the rated power. The resistor will overheat, the color bands will turn brown/black, and eventually the resistor burns open (fails as open circuit).

Fix options: - Use a higher resistance (1kО©: P = 144mW вң“) - Use a higher-power resistor (2W wire-wound) - Use a different approach entirely (voltage regulator)

Rule of thumb: Always calculate power dissipation. Derate by 50% (don’t run a 1/4W resistor above 1/8W for long life).

    R_total = R1 + R2 + R3

Intuition: Three narrow sections of highway in a row. Cars must pass through all three. Total narrowing = sum of all narrowing.

Parallel: Conductances ADD (reciprocals)

         в”Ңв”Җв”Җ R1 в”Җв”Җв”җ
    в”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Ө        в”ңв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җ

    1/R_total = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3

Intuition: Three highways side by side. Cars can take any of the three. More routes = less total resistance. The result is ALWAYS less than the smallest individual resistor.

Special case (two resistors in parallel):

    R_total = (R1 Г— R2) / (R1 + R2)

Quick mental math: Two equal resistors in parallel = half of one. 10kО© вҲҘ 10kО© = 5kО©.

PART 2 вҖ” CAPACITORS

2.1 What a Capacitor Physically IS

A capacitor is two conductive plates separated by an insulator (dielectric). Apply voltage вҶ’ electrons pile up on one plate and are repelled from the other. This stores energy in the electric field between the plates.

The water tank analogy (detailed mapping):

    WATER MODEL                         ELECTRICAL MODEL
    в”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җ                         в”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җ

    Pipe resistance               вҶҗвҶ’    Series resistance (ESR)

    Filling a tank through a narrow pipe:

    - As tank fills, back-pressure builds           вҶ’ voltage rises, current decreases
    - Eventually, pressure equalizes вҶ’ flow stops   вҶ’ capacitor fully charged, current = 0

Key equations:

    Q = C Г— V        (charge stored = capacitance Г— voltage)
    I = C Г— dV/dt    (current = capacitance Г— rate of voltage change)

That second equation is the important one. It says: current only flows through a capacitor when its voltage is CHANGING. A steady (DC) voltage вҶ’ no current flows. A changing (AC) voltage вҶ’ current flows proportional to how fast it changes.

2.2 Why Capacitors Block DC but Pass AC

From I = C Г— dV/dt: - DC (constant voltage): dV/dt = 0 вҶ’ I = 0. No current flows. The capacitor looks like an open circuit. - AC (changing voltage): dV/dt is nonzero вҶ’ current flows. Higher frequency = faster change = more current.

Capacitive reactance (impedance):

    Xc = 1 / (2ПҖ Г— f Г— C)

    Where:
    Xc = impedance in Ohms (how much the cap "resists" current)
    f  = frequency in Hz
    C  = capacitance in Farads

Real numbers: | Capacitor | @ 100Hz | @ 10kHz | @ 1MHz | |-----------|---------|---------|--------| | 100nF | 15.9kО© | 159О© | 1.6О© | | 10ВөF | 159О© | 1.6О© | 0.016О© |

At DC (f=0), Xc = вҲһ (open circuit). At high frequency, Xc вҶ’ 0 (short circuit). This is why capacitors are AC “pass-through” and DC “blockers.”

2.3 Decoupling Capacitors вҖ” The Most Important Capacitor in Your Circuit

Why should I care? If your SPI bridge randomly corrupts data or the MCU resets under heavy load, the #1 suspect is inadequate decoupling.

The Problem

When a digital IC switches its outputs, it draws a burst of current from VCC. On an STM32H7 running at 480MHz, these bursts happen billions of times per second, each lasting nanoseconds.

The power supply is far away (centimeters of PCB trace). Traces have inductance (~1nH per mm). Inductance resists changes in current (V = L Г— dI/dt). So when the IC demands current NOW, the trace inductance prevents it from arriving instantly. Result: VCC sags for a few nanoseconds.

Ceramic cap gotcha (X7R/X5R): The capacitance drops significantly with applied DC voltage. A “10ВөF” X5R cap rated at 6.3V may only be 5ВөF when you apply 3.3V across it, and 2ВөF at 5V. Always check the datasheet derating curve. The “no instantaneous voltage change” rule, I = C Г— dV/dt, means the capacitor acts like a short circuit for sudden voltage spikes (dV/dt is enormous вҶ’ huge current flows). This is exactly what makes decoupling work вҖ” the cap absorbs the voltage spike.

2.6 The “No Instantaneous Voltage Change” Rule

From I = C Г— dV/dt: if voltage changed infinitely fast (dt вҶ’ 0), current would be infinite. Since infinite current can’t exist, the voltage CANNOT change instantaneously.

Consequences: 1. Capacitors smooth out voltage spikes (decoupling) 2. You can’t teleport a capacitor from 0V to 5V вҖ” it charges through whatever resistance is in the path 3. Short-circuiting a charged capacitor вҶ’ enormous current pulse (the cap tries to maintain its voltage, current shoots up to equalize). This can weld contacts and destroy components.

Practical rule for your SPI bridge: Never hot-plug a board with large capacitors into a powered bus. The rush of current charging the caps can damage the source or the connector.

PART 3 вҖ” INDUCTORS

3.1 What an Inductor Physically IS

An inductor is a coil of wire. When current flows through it, it creates a magnetic field. Energy is stored in this magnetic field.

The flywheel analogy (detailed mapping):

    FLYWHEEL MODEL                      ELECTRICAL MODEL
    в”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җ                      в”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җ
    Flywheel (spinning mass)      вҶҗвҶ’    Inductor (coil)
    Rotation speed                вҶҗвҶ’    Current (Amps)
    Torque (force to speed up)    вҶҗвҶ’    Voltage (Volts)
    Inertia (mass Г— radiusВІ)     вҶҗвҶ’    Inductance (Henrys)

    Key behavior:
    - Heavy flywheel resists speeding up            вҶ’ inductor resists current increase
    - Heavy flywheel resists slowing down            вҶ’ inductor resists current decrease
    - You CAN'T instantly stop a spinning flywheel  вҶ’ you CAN'T instantly stop inductor current
      (it stores kinetic energy)                      (it stores magnetic energy)

    Where the analogy breaks down:
    - A flywheel eventually stops (friction)
    - An ideal inductor would maintain current forever (superconducting coil really does)

Key equation:

    V = L Г— dI/dt

    Where:
    V = voltage across the inductor (Volts)
    L = inductance (Henrys)
    dI/dt = rate of change of current (Amps/second)

Compare this with the capacitor equation I = C Г— dV/dt. They’re duals: - Capacitor: resists voltage change, stores energy in electric field - Inductor: resists current change, stores energy in magnetic field

3.2 DC vs AC Behavior вҖ” Opposite of Capacitors

At DC (steady current): dI/dt = 0 вҶ’ V = 0. The inductor looks like a short circuit (just a piece of wire). Current flows freely.

At AC (changing current): dI/dt is nonzero вҶ’ voltage develops across the inductor. Higher frequency = faster change = more opposition.

Inductive reactance:

    XL = 2ПҖ Г— f Г— L

    Opposite of capacitive reactance!
Component DC Behavior AC Behavior
Capacitor Open circuit (blocks DC) Passes AC (lower impedance at higher freq)
Inductor Short circuit (passes DC) Blocks AC (higher impedance at higher freq)

This complementary behavior is why LC combinations make such good filters.

3.3 Back-EMF вҖ” Why Switching Off an Inductive Load is Dangerous

Why should I care? If your robot drives relays, solenoids, or motors from MCU GPIOs, you MUST understand back-EMF or you WILL destroy transistors.

From V = L Г— dI/dt: if you try to change current VERY FAST (like turning off a switch), dI/dt becomes enormous, so V becomes enormous вҖ” potentially hundreds of volts from a 12V relay coil!

Now when the switch opens, current circulates through the diode + coil, safely decaying as the energy dissipates in the coil’s resistance. The voltage spike is clamped to ~0.7V (the diode forward drop).

Why should I care? The Jetson Orin and STM32 both use DC-DC converters internally. Understanding why they need inductors helps you understand switching noise вҖ” a major source of SPI interference.

A buck converter (step-down) uses a switch + inductor + capacitor to efficiently convert 12V to 3.3V:

3.5 Ferrite Beads вҖ” The Inductor You Use Every Day

A ferrite bead is a special inductor designed to be lossy at high frequencies вҖ” it converts noise to heat instead of storing and releasing energy.

    Schematic symbol:

    в”Җв”Җв”ӨFBв”ңв”Җв”Җ    or    в”Җв”Җв”ӨLFBв”ңв”Җв”Җ

Used on power rails to block high-frequency switching noise while passing DC freely. In your project, you’ll likely see them on the STM32 VDDA (analog power) pin, filtering noise from the main 3.3V rail.

PART 4 вҖ” RLC CIRCUITS AND FILTERS

4.1 The Big Picture: Frequency-Selective Circuits

By combining R, L, and C, you can build circuits that pass some frequencies and block others. These are filters, and they’re everywhere in electronics.

4.2 Low-Pass Filter (RC)

Passes low frequencies, blocks high frequencies. The simplest: one resistor + one capacitor.

    Input в”Җв”Җв”Ө R в”ңв”Җв”Җв”¬в”Җв”Җ Output
                   в”Ӯ
                  в”Ңв”ҙв”җ
                  в”ӮCв”Ӯ
                  в””в”¬в”ҳ
                   в”Ӯ
    GND в”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”ҳ

    Cutoff frequency: f_c = 1 / (2ПҖ Г— R Г— C)

At the cutoff frequency, the output is 70.7% of the input (-3dB).

Worked Example: Anti-alias filter for ADC

Your ADC samples at 1kHz. Nyquist says you must filter out everything above 500Hz.

Design for f_c = 500Hz:

    Choose C = 100nF (common value)
    R = 1 / (2ПҖ Г— 500 Г— 100Г—10вҒ»вҒ№) = 1 / (0.000314) = 3.18kО© вҶ’ use 3.3kО©

Check: f_c = 1 / (2ПҖ Г— 3300 Г— 100Г—10вҒ»вҒ№) = 482Hz вң“ (close enough)

This is a first-order filter: -20dB/decade rolloff (factor of 10 attenuation per decade of frequency above f_c). For steeper rolloff, cascade multiple stages or use op-amp active filters.

4.3 High-Pass Filter (RC)

Passes high frequencies, blocks low frequencies. Swap R and C:

    Input в”Җв”Җв”Ө C в”ңв”Җв”Җв”¬в”Җв”Җ Output
                   в”Ӯ

    Same cutoff formula: f_c = 1 / (2ПҖ Г— R Г— C)

Used for: AC coupling (removing DC offset from audio signals), differentiating signals.

4.4 Series RLC and Resonance

When L and C are in series, there’s a special frequency where their impedances cancel out:

    f_resonant = 1 / (2ПҖ Г— вҲҡ(L Г— C))

At resonance: - XL = XC (inductive and capacitive reactances are equal) - They cancel, leaving only R - Current is maximum (only limited by R) - This is how radio tuners work вҖ” the LC circuit “selects” one frequency

Quality factor Q:

    Q = 1/R Г— вҲҡ(L/C)     (for series RLC)
  • High Q: sharp, narrow resonance peak (good for selecting one radio station)
  • Low Q: broad, gentle peak (good for wideband filters)

C1 filters high-frequency noise, L blocks remaining mid-frequency noise, C2 smooths the final output. Used on analog power rails (VDDA, VREF) to keep switching noise from DC-DC converters away from sensitive ADCs.
